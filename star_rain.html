Discover

————————
As a Member of the Autism Services Kids Club

While volunteering, I came to realize that mainstream therapy for children with autism in Taiwan still relies on traditional means, such as face-to-face instruction and medical treatment. I felt strongly that alternative learning methods like educational apps should be leveraged, since many kids showed interest in interacting with digital devices.

As a UX designer, I keep reflecting on the question: how might we help children with autism and their families to ease the daily struggle and improve their lives? It inspired me to conduct a UX design project to address the possibility that children with autism could enhance their learning abilities through technology.

Design Process

————————

The half-year long design process contained the research phase and two design iterations. I did the observatory during the volunteer activities, interviewed with autistic kids, parents and professionals in the first month, and the rest of the design period happened at my class of the User Experience Design.

Interview

————————
Understand the problems and difficulties

We started with user interviews in order to understand the problems that kids with autism and their parents faced in daily life, including 5 kids, 8 parents, 2 teachers and 3 doctors.

Key findings from interviews:

1. Many kids show interest in interacting with mobile phones and computers.
2. Kids often forget or lose their stuff. Thus, the caregivers have to check and remind him/her many times each day.
3. Teaching kids with autism to learn living skills is very exhausted. Parents desire to have effective methods.
4. With proper methods, kids can significantly improve their abilities.
5. Though special education at school provides classes for living skills and social interaction, there is still a huge difference when they face the real world.
6. The autistic symptoms vary widely from one person to the next. It will be suitable if kids can be taught by the one who understands him/her the most.

User Journey Map: Potential Entrypoints

————————

The results from interviews shed light on our design directions. To analyze our research and identify problem space, we drew out a user journey and saw potential entry points.

Key entry points can mainly distinct into two dimensions:
1. Social Interaction Difficulties: There is no fixed pattern for social dysfunction because kids on the autism spectrum are unique with different strengths and need at different ages. Yet, most of them have obvious difficulties in social interaction.
2. Lack of Living Skills: Learning practical skills can be challenging for kids with autism, but is essential for independence (For example, shopping, taking transportation, completing daily chores, house cleaning, doing dishes and cooking).

Persona: Kids with Autism and Their Parents

————————

According to the journey map, we found that there were two issues: kids with autism lack life skills, and parents grow exhausted from having to repeatedly teach them things. Therefore, we designed for two kinds of users: kids with autism and their parents.

Designing a user persona for Eddie Wu and his parents helped us in making informed design decisions as we had the end-users always in mind.

Competitor Analysis

————————

We analyzed 20 existing education apps for kids, including learning the alphabet, math, geometry, draw, and music. There were also a few apps designed for learning life skills, like go shopping and take a bus. However, there were two main problems of existing education tools:
1. Kids can not connect teaching and gaming contents with the real world.
2. Children with autism have individual differences. Each one should be taught and treated by distinct methods. It can make sure that kids receive proper supports and reach person-centered needs.

Thus, we picked fidelity level and customized level as competitor analysis standards. We wanted to design an education app that caregivers can customize teaching content, and through real-life scenarios, kids can learn living skills effectively.

Design Concept

————————

1. Gamification: Through gamification, fun can be put back in the learning equation for kids and increase engagement.
2. Real-life scenarios: Each stage displays with real-life scenarios, which helps kids connect learning contents with the real world.
3. Customized-teaching content: Parents can customize teaching contents for their kids, such as riding a school bus and shopping in a convenience store.

Hide-Fidelity Design

————————

What did we learn from the prototpye testing?

————————

At first, we designed a prototype and conducted usability tests both for desktop and app. We found that kids with autism were easier to use an app rather than desktop because some kids had muscle weakness when they controlled the mouses. Later, we shifted focus to the usability tests for mobile devices.

We conducted fifteen minutes long 1:1 session with 5 kids and 7 parents. After testing, parents did a series of qualitative questions regarding the prototype's interface and functionality. The overall impression was positive and we got insightful feedback. Kids played the games with enthusiastic, and parents loved the features of customizing. The design was able to reduce caregivers' feeling of fatigue when they taught kids repeatedly.

Nevertheless, some parents thought that it won't be attractive for teenagers with autism, and had limited effect. Therefore, it still needs to follow up for a piece of time to value the effect of the design solution and optimize the idea later.

Reflection and Takeaways

————————

During the design process, I maintained close connection with the foundation for autistic children and adults in Taiwan, a non-profit organization helping autistic people to deserve a better life. They gave me several feedback based on their professional experience and helped me iterate the design to tackle the real issues.

Through the process of iteration, I learned how to reach the autistic kids’ market in the digital world, how to improve a product’s usability, and how to place myself in a user’s shoes objectively and comprehensively. I appreciated to have this opportunity and truely understood their needings and pain points. By solving this trivial stuff happened in their every day, I hope that the Star Rain can eventually provide a better life experience.
